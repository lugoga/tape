Where Are We? The Tanga-Pemba Seascape

A transboundary ecoregion connecting mainland Tanzania and Pemba Island.

  • Globally Significant: A hotspot of marine biodiversity in the Western Indian Ocean.
  • Connected Habitats: A mosaic of coral reefs, seagrass beds, and mangrove forests.
  • Economic Engine: The heart of our coastal economy and culture.
  • Shared Resource: Managed by both mainland Tanzania and Zanzibar authorities.

A Treasure of Biodiversity

This seascape is home to globally important and protected species.

Key Habitats

  • Coral Reefs: Some of the most resilient in the region, acting as fish nurseries and coastal protection.
  • Seagrass Beds: Critical feeding grounds for dugongs and sea turtles.
  • Mangrove Forests: Vital nurseries for fish and powerful “blue carbon” sinks.
  • The Pemba Channel: A deep-water migratory corridor for marine giants.

Flagship Species

  • Coelacanth (CR): The “living fossil” found in deep canyons.
  • Dugong (VU): Relies on our healthy seagrass.
  • Sea Turtles (EN, CR): Nest on our beaches.
  • Whale Shark (EN): Migrates through our waters.
  • Humpback Whale (LC): Breeds and calves here.

IUCN Red List Status: CR=Critically Endangered, EN=Endangered, VU=Vulnerable

The Heart of Our Communities

This is not just nature; it’s our home and our livelihood.

  • Fisheries: The primary source of protein and income for over 350,000 people. Mostly small-scale, artisanal fishers.

  • Seaweed Farming: A vital alternative livelihood, especially empowering women in coastal communities.

  • Tourism: A growing sector with potential for sustainable ecotourism (diving, whale watching).

  • Coastal Protection: Healthy reefs and mangroves protect our villages from storms and erosion.

The health of the sea is directly linked to the well-being of our families.

Facing Shared Challenges

Our shared resource is under pressure from multiple directions.

Local Pressures

  • Overfishing: Too many boats chasing too few fish.
  • Destructive Practices: Dynamite fishing and illegal nets destroy the very foundation of our fisheries.
  • Habitat Loss: Mangroves are cleared for development and salt pans.
  • Pollution: Runoff from land harms sensitive marine life.

::: {.column width-sooner width=“50%”} ### Global Pressures

  • Climate Change:
    • Rising sea temperatures cause coral bleaching.
    • Sea-level rise threatens coastal communities and habitats.
    • Ocean acidification weakens coral skeletons.

:::

A Vision for the Future: Man and the Biosphere

We propose a path forward inspired by the UNESCO “Man and the Biosphere” (MAB) model.

The Goal: Balance conservation with sustainable human development.

  1. Conservation: Protect critical habitats and species for future generations.
    • Example: Well-managed “no-take” zones (Core Areas).
  2. Development: Foster economic activities that support communities without harming the environment.
    • Example: Sustainable fisheries, ecotourism, and value-added seaweed farming (Transition Areas).
  3. Collaboration: Use science, research, and local knowledge to guide our decisions together.
    • Example: Joint patrols and community-led monitoring (Logistic Support).

Our Strengths: What We Are Already Doing

We are not starting from zero. Great work is already underway.

  • Marine Protected Areas (MPAs): Tanga Coelacanth Marine Park (TCMP) and Pemba Channel Conservation Area (PECCA) are our conservation cornerstones.

  • Community-Led Management (CFMAs): Empowered local communities are setting rules, patrolling their waters, and managing their resources.

  • Alternative Livelihoods: Projects in beekeeping, poultry, and improved seaweed processing are reducing pressure on the sea.

  • Strong Partnerships: NGOs, government bodies, and research institutions are already working together.

Our challenge is to connect these efforts and scale them up across the entire seascape.

The Path Forward: Discussion Points

How can we work together to build a sustainable and prosperous future for the Tanga-Pemba Seascape?

  1. Strengthening Governance: How can we improve coordination between mainland and Zanzibar authorities for more effective management?

  2. Empowering Communities: What additional support do local management groups (CFMAs) need to succeed? (e.g., resources, legal backing, training)

  3. Creating Sustainable Economies: How can we best support and grow businesses (tourism, fisheries) that are good for both people and nature?

  4. Tackling Illegal Activities: What new strategies can we use to finally stop destructive fishing practices?
